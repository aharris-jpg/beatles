The States Where You're Most Likely to Hear a Beatles Song (2026)

We put the Fab Four's legacy to the ultimate odds test — state by state, song by song.

All You Need Is Odds

Here at RotoGrinders, we calculate odds for a living. So when Global Beatles Day rolled around on June 25, we asked the question that only an odds team would think to ask: what are the actual odds of hearing a Beatles song in your state?

We pulled average monthly Google search volumes for 28 Beatles-specific keywords and 8 broad music benchmark terms across all 50 states, covering the most recent 12 months of available data. Divide one by the other and you get a clean implied probability — the likelihood that any random music search in that state is Beatles-related.

The answer tells you exactly where Beatles music still dominates the cultural conversation. And the results are not what you'd expect from a band that released its last record over 50 years ago.

Vermont leads the country at 2.92%. That means roughly 1 in every 34 music searches in the state is about The Beatles. The entire top four are small New England states. Mississippi sits last at 0.64%. The gap between first and last? 4.6 times. For a band that has been part of the American soundtrack since 1964, that is a striking spread.

Let it be known: the Beatles are alive and well in Vermont.

Key Findings

  • Vermont ranks #1 at 2.92% — roughly 1 in every 34 music searches in the state is Beatles-related, the highest concentration in the country.
  • The entire top four are New England states — Vermont, New Hampshire, Maine, and Rhode Island all outrank New York, California, and every Sun Belt state.
  • "Here Comes The Sun" dominates 43 of 50 states — the most-searched Beatles track nationally by a wide margin, with only 7 states breaking the pattern.
  • Vermont outperforms New York by more than 2 times (2.92% vs. 1.38%), despite New York having roughly 30 times the population.
  • Mississippi ranks last at 0.64% — the gap between #1 Vermont and #50 Mississippi is 4.6 times, the widest spread in the dataset.
  • Nevada is the only western state where "Hey Jude" tops the chart — every other western state defaults to "Here Comes The Sun."

The Song Map: Where Beatles Classics Still Divide America

"Here Comes The Sun" wins 43 of 50 states. But 7 states break the pattern — and the splits reveal something about regional identity that the headline rankings alone cannot show.

"Let It Be" clusters in the Deep South and Hawaii. Alabama, Louisiana, Wisconsin, and Hawaii all return "Let It Be" as their top Beatles search. Three of those four states rank below the national average on overall Beatles engagement, which makes the song choice more telling. The listeners who do search are reaching for something specific — something warmer, more gospel-inflected, more direct.

"Penny Lane" surfaces in Virginia and Wyoming. Both states trend toward deeper catalogue knowledge. "Penny Lane" is not the track casual fans reach for first — you have to know your Beatles to land there over "Here Comes The Sun."

Nevada stands alone as the only western state where "Hey Jude" tops the chart. Las Vegas's live entertainment industry almost certainly drives that signal. "Hey Jude" is an anthem built for large rooms and singalongs — Nevada's tourism-driven search behavior reflects exactly that.

The Needle Still Drops Where It Always Has

Vermont at 2.92% and Mississippi at 0.64% are 4.6 times apart in implied probability. Both are generating real Beatles search volume 60 years after the band's US debut.

The top of the rankings belongs to small, geographically isolated states that have resisted the churn of streaming-era music discovery. New England holds all four top spots. Three western states with populations under 2 million crack the top 10 ahead of California, Texas, and Florida.

For media buyers and catalogue placement budgets, the state-by-state spread is actionable. The states with the tightest odds are not the biggest markets — they are the most concentrated ones. Vermont at +3321 is a very different audience to New York at +7164, even though New York generates more raw search volume in absolute terms.

Six decades of streaming, algorithm-driven playlists, and catalogue competition have not flattened the map. The data covers June 2025 through May 2026 and the pattern holds. Small, older, less algorithmically penetrated markets punch well above their weight — and the gap is not closing.

The Beatles are still playing in Vermont. They just never really stopped.

Methodology

What we measured: The implied probability of hearing a Beatles song in each US state, defined as the share of music-related Google searches that are Beatles-specific. Expressed as an implied probability, fractional odds, and American odds.

Source and date range: Google Keyword Planner, average monthly search volumes, June 2025 through May 2026. Google Keyword Planner was used rather than Google Trends because Trends returns only a relative index (0-100), which cannot produce a meaningful ratio. Keyword Planner returns actual search volumes that divide cleanly against each other.

Keywords: 28 Beatles-specific keywords across four categories (artist, song, lyrics, playlist) were divided by the sum of 8 broad music benchmark keywords (music, songs, lyrics, playlist, albums, music videos, song lyrics, new songs). Four song titles — Yesterday, Something, Help, and Blackbird — were qualified with "Beatles" appended to avoid capturing unrelated searches; their volumes are conservative lower-bound estimates. One data quality issue was identified and corrected: the initial West Virginia export contained Washington state data and was replaced with a corrected pull.

Formula: Implied probability = sum of Beatles keyword volumes divided by sum of benchmark keyword volumes, multiplied by 100. Fractional odds = 1 divided by that share. American odds = (1 divided by share, minus 1) multiplied by 100.

Exclusions: The District of Columbia was excluded by design. All 50 US states are included. Keyword Planner volumes are rounded averages; small-state figures are rounded to the nearest 10 or 100, standard across all public keyword tools. Population source: US Census Bureau state population estimates, January 7, 2025. Data extracted June 2026.
